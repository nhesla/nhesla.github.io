// Dev Notes
What went well

The asset pipeline came together nicely — I sourced tank sprites, environment tiles, and explosion sheets from separate places, modified them to fit, and got them feeling cohesive. The controls also ended up feeling pretty tight for a two-week project: WASD movement, mouse aim, and a jump mechanic that interacts with the procedurally generated terrain elevation.

What I learned

The most interesting technical piece was implementing a bitmask tileset for terrain generation. The idea is that each tile checks its neighbors and picks the correct sprite based on which sides are bordered — so terrain edges, corners, and open faces all render correctly without hardcoding every case. It's a clean pattern for this kind of problem and I'd use it again.

Mistakes & what I'd do differently

The map generation works for the purposes of this project but doesn't incorporate any logic into terrain placement and would be unsuited for a larger game. I'd like to try wave function collapse next time, which would let me design actual tile rules and get maps that feel hand-crafted even when they're procedurally generated.

The static foliage sprites are also something I'd fix. Even a simple two or three frame animation on the grass and bushes would make the environment feel less like wallpaper.

The bigger issue is scope. One environment, one screen's worth of space, and enemies that only differ in color and health — it gets repetitive fast. Weapon pickups, a stomp kill animation, or even just some screenshake would have added a lot of personality for relatively little work. I'd also want multiple maps or at least a scrolling play area to give the game more room to breathe.
